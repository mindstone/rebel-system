# Tracing Knowledge Lineages

Map how an idea, strategy, or approach has evolved over time — through conversations, documents, decisions, and external influences. Understanding where thinking came from makes it easier to see where it should go next.

## See also

- [`knowledge-extraction-report`](../knowledge-extraction-report/SKILL.md) — synthesising multiple documents
- [`meta-pattern-recognition`](../../thinking/meta-pattern-recognition/SKILL.md) — spotting recurring patterns

## [GOAL]

Help the user understand the lineage of an idea — its origins, how it changed, what influenced it, and what was lost or gained along the way — so they can make more informed decisions about its future direction.

## [PROCESS]

1. **Identify the idea to trace**
   - Ask: "What concept, strategy, or approach do you want to trace the history of?"
   - Clarify scope: "How far back should we go? And where should we look?" (conversations, documents, memory, external sources)

2. **Gather the trail**
   - Search available sources for mentions, discussions, and decisions related to the idea:
     - Memory and previous conversations
     - Documents and files
     - Emails and messages (if MCP available)
     - Meeting transcripts
     - External influences (articles, books, competitors, advisors)
   - Build a timeline of the idea's evolution

3. **Map the lineage**
   - For each significant iteration of the idea, note:
     - **What changed** from the previous version
     - **Why it changed** (new information, different stakeholder, failure of previous version, external influence)
     - **What was gained** in the change
     - **What was lost** in the change (features, simplicity, original intent, stakeholder buy-in)
   - Identify the key inflection points where the idea shifted direction

4. **Analyse the evolution**
   - Is the idea converging (getting clearer and more focused) or diverging (getting broader and more complex)?
   - What assumptions from the original version are still present? Are they still valid?
   - What external influences shaped it most? Were those influences still relevant?
   - Has anything been lost along the way that should be recovered?

5. **Inform the next iteration**
   - Based on the lineage: "Here's what the evolution suggests about where this idea is heading"
   - Identify unexamined assumptions carried from earlier versions
   - Suggest what should change and what should be preserved
   - Ask: "Knowing this history, what would you do differently?"

## [IMPORTANT]

- This is detective work, not judgment. The goal is to surface the history, not to critique past decisions.
- Be specific about sources and dates. "In your January email to the board..." is better than "at some point you mentioned..."
- Ideas rarely evolve linearly. Expect dead ends, reversals, and parallel threads.
- If sources are limited, be honest about gaps: "I can trace this from [date] but not further back."
- Particularly useful for: strategy evolution, product vision drift, policy changes, recurring debates that never quite get resolved.
