# Preserving Productive Tensions

Some decisions don't have a right answer — they have trade-offs between competing values that both matter. Speed vs quality. Growth vs sustainability. Autonomy vs alignment. This skill helps hold those tensions productively instead of prematurely picking a side.

## See also

- [`sounding-board-mode`](../sounding-board-mode/SKILL.md) — open-ended advisory
- [`devils-advocate`](../devils-advocate/SKILL.md) — structured critique

## [GOAL]

Help the user navigate decisions where the tension between competing priorities is itself the useful thing — and collapsing it too early would lose information.

## [PROCESS]

1. **Name the tension**
   - Ask: "What's the decision you're wrestling with?"
   - Help articulate it as a genuine tension between two valuable things, not a right-vs-wrong choice
   - Format: "[Value A] vs [Value B]" — e.g., "moving fast vs getting buy-in", "standardisation vs team autonomy", "short-term revenue vs long-term brand"

2. **Steelman both sides**
   - For each side of the tension, build the strongest possible case
   - Ask: "What's the best argument for prioritising [side A]?"
   - Ask: "What's the best argument for prioritising [side B]?"
   - Ensure the user genuinely sees the merit in both before proceeding

3. **Map the context**
   - "When in your situation is [side A] more important?"
   - "When is [side B] more important?"
   - Often the answer isn't either/or — it's a question of context, timing, or scope
   - Identify the variables that determine which side should dominate in a given moment

4. **Design the holding pattern**
   - Instead of resolving the tension, help design a system for managing it:
     - **Decision rules**: "Default to [A], escalate to [B] when [condition]"
     - **Time-boxing**: "Prioritise [A] for the next quarter, then reassess"
     - **Scope-splitting**: "[A] for this product line, [B] for that one"
     - **Explicit review**: "We'll revisit this tension every [interval]"
   - The goal is a clear, articulable policy — not a vague "it depends"

5. **Set the tripwires**
   - "What signal would tell you the balance has tipped too far toward [A]?"
   - "What signal would tell you [B] is being neglected?"
   - These become early warning indicators so the tension stays productive

## [IMPORTANT]

- The instinct to "just decide" is strong but sometimes premature. Some tensions are permanent features of the landscape, not problems to solve.
- This is not about avoiding decisions. It's about making better decisions by understanding which tensions to preserve and which to resolve.
- Ask 1-2 questions at a time.
- If one side of the tension is clearly more important, say so. Not every decision is a genuine tension.
- Works well for: org design decisions, product strategy trade-offs, hiring philosophy, culture questions, leadership style.
