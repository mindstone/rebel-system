# Collision Zone Thinking

Deliberately combine concepts from unrelated domains to generate novel ideas, solutions, or perspectives. Innovation often happens at the intersection of fields that don't normally talk to each other.

## See also

- [`sounding-board-mode`](../sounding-board-mode/SKILL.md) — open-ended advisory
- [`meta-pattern-recognition`](../meta-pattern-recognition/SKILL.md) — finding patterns across domains

## [GOAL]

Help the user generate genuinely novel ideas by deliberately colliding concepts from different domains, industries, or disciplines.

## [PROCESS]

1. **Define the challenge**
   - Ask the user to describe the problem, opportunity, or area where they want fresh thinking
   - Clarify what "good" would look like — are they seeking a new product idea, a strategy, a way to communicate something, a process improvement?

2. **Identify the home domain**
   - Name the domain the challenge lives in (e.g., "enterprise sales", "team management", "customer onboarding")
   - List the standard approaches, metaphors, and assumptions in that domain

3. **Select collision domains**
   - Pick 2-3 domains that are deliberately distant from the home domain
   - Good collision domains: biology, architecture, game design, hospitality, military strategy, improvisational theatre, sports coaching, urban planning, restaurant kitchens, jazz ensembles
   - Ask the user: "What are you interested in outside work?" — personal interests make the best collision material because they carry real knowledge

4. **Collide**
   - For each collision domain, ask: "How does [collision domain] solve a problem like yours?"
   - Extract principles, structures, or mechanisms from the collision domain
   - Map them onto the original challenge: "What would it look like if your [challenge] worked like [collision domain principle]?"
   - Don't filter too early — capture all ideas, even absurd ones

5. **Harvest**
   - Review what emerged. Which collisions produced genuine insight?
   - For the best ideas, pressure-test: "Is this actually useful, or just clever?"
   - Identify 1-3 ideas worth exploring further
   - Define the next step for each

## [IMPORTANT]

- The best collisions feel slightly uncomfortable or absurd at first. That's the signal, not the noise.
- Don't explain the technique too much upfront — just do it. "How would a jazz ensemble handle your hiring problem?" is more productive than a lecture on lateral thinking.
- If the user gravitates toward safe, obvious connections, push them further afield.
- Ask 1-2 questions at a time.
- Not every collision produces gold. Three attempts yielding one good idea is a strong result.
