# Inversion Exercise

Instead of thinking about how to achieve a goal, invert it: ask how you would guarantee the opposite outcome. This exposes blind spots, hidden risks, and assumed constraints that normal forward-thinking misses.

Inspired by Charlie Munger: "Invert, always invert."

## See also

- [`devils-advocate`](../devils-advocate/SKILL.md) — structured critique (uses inversion as one drill)
- [`scale-game`](../scale-game/SKILL.md) — stress-testing at extremes

## [GOAL]

Help the user see their situation from the opposite direction, revealing risks, assumptions, and safeguards they would otherwise miss.

## [PROCESS]

1. **State the goal**
   - Ask: "What are you trying to achieve?"
   - Get a clear, specific answer (not "make things better" — what specifically?)

2. **Invert it**
   - Ask: "If you wanted to guarantee the opposite — to make this fail spectacularly — what would you do?"
   - Push for specifics: "What decisions, behaviours, or conditions would make failure certain?"
   - Aim for 5-10 failure strategies

3. **Check for accidental inversion**
   - Review the failure list and ask: "Are you currently doing any of these, even partially?"
   - This is where the real insight lives — people are often unconsciously pursuing some of the failure strategies
   - Be direct: "Number 4 on your failure list — 'ignore customer feedback' — how close is your current process to that?"

4. **Extract safeguards**
   - For each failure strategy, derive the corresponding safeguard or practice
   - "If 'hire fast without checking culture fit' guarantees failure, what's the corresponding positive practice?"
   - Prioritise: which safeguards are missing or weak in the current plan?

5. **Update the plan**
   - What specific changes does the inversion suggest?
   - Which risks were invisible before and are now visible?
   - What's the single most important safeguard to put in place?

## [IMPORTANT]

- The power is in step 3 — checking whether you're accidentally doing the things that guarantee failure. Don't rush past it.
- Keep the tone light and exploratory. "How would you blow this up?" is more productive when it feels like a game rather than an audit.
- Ask 1-2 questions at a time.
- Works for business strategy, hiring, product launches, personal goals, relationship decisions — any domain where you have a clear desired outcome.
