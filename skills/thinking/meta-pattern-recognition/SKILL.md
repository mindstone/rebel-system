# Meta-Pattern Recognition

Spot the deeper patterns that recur across different domains, projects, or situations. The same structural shape often appears in team dynamics, market strategy, product design, and personal decisions.

## See also

- [`coaching-conversation`](../coaching-conversation/SKILL.md) — evidence-based pattern discovery in personal behaviour
- [`sounding-board-mode`](../sounding-board-mode/SKILL.md) — open-ended advisory

## [GOAL]

Help the user recognise structural patterns that repeat across their work and life, and use that recognition to make better decisions faster.

## [PROCESS]

1. **Gather examples**
   - Ask the user to describe 2-3 situations, problems, or decisions they're dealing with (they can be from completely different areas)
   - Listen for structural similarities they haven't noticed yet

2. **Extract the shape**
   - For each situation, reduce it to its structural elements:
     - Who are the actors and what are their incentives?
     - What's the tension or trade-off at the core?
     - What's the typical progression or lifecycle?
   - Name the pattern when you see it: "This looks like a [coordination problem / tragedy of the commons / local maximum trap / trust deficit / scaling bottleneck]"

3. **Cross-reference**
   - Ask: "Where else in your work or life do you see this same shape?"
   - Look across domains: team dynamics, customer relationships, product decisions, personal habits
   - The more distant the domains, the more powerful the recognition

4. **Draw insight from the pattern**
   - If the pattern is known (e.g., principal-agent problem, innovator's dilemma), share what others have learned about navigating it
   - Ask: "Now that you can see this is the same pattern, what does that change about how you'd approach it?"
   - Identify which interventions address the pattern itself vs just one instance

5. **Build pattern literacy**
   - Help the user name their pattern for future recognition
   - Ask: "What would be your early warning signal that this pattern is forming again?"
   - Suggest a simple heuristic or checklist for catching it earlier next time

## [IMPORTANT]

- Don't force patterns where they don't exist. Not everything is connected.
- Name patterns in plain language, not jargon. "This is a trust gap" beats "this is a principal-agent problem" for most people.
- The user's own recognition is more valuable than your pattern-matching. Guide, don't lecture.
- Ask 1-2 questions at a time.
- Search the web for relevant frameworks or research if a pattern warrants deeper context.
