# Write Intro Email

Draft professional mutual introduction emails connecting two people who could benefit from knowing each other.

## When to Use

- Connecting two contacts who could benefit from knowing each other
- Someone asks for a specific introduction or referral
- You identify a valuable connection opportunity
- Following up on a promise to introduce someone

## Before You Write: Double Opt-In

**Always use double opt-in** unless the user explicitly says otherwise. This is the gold standard for professional introductions (popularised by Fred Wilson, Union Square Ventures).

### The Process

1. **Decide if the intro makes sense.** Is there a compelling reason for *both* people? If one person only wants a favour, think about what value they bring to the other.
2. **Ask the right person first.** Check with the person you've known longer, have a deeper relationship with, or who is more senior. If they decline, the other person never knows.
3. **Provide context in the permission request.** Include: who the person is (LinkedIn URL if possible), how you know them, what the ask is, and why it's valuable *for the recipient specifically*.
4. **Give people an easy out.** Use phrasing like: *"If for any reason you aren't into it, just tell me or ignore this — I completely understand."*
5. **Only proceed if both agree.**

### Pre-Introduction Check Email Template

```
Hi [Name],

[1-2 lines about the other person and why you're thinking of connecting them]

Would you be up for an introduction?

[sign-off]
```

## The Introduction Email

### Core Structure

Every introduction email follows this structure:

1. **Subject line** — Clear and searchable
2. **Opening greeting** — Name both parties
3. **Opening line** — Brief, warm
4. **Person A description** — 1-2 paragraphs with context
5. **Person B description** — Balanced treatment
6. **Context / rationale** — Why this connection makes sense
7. **Handoff** — Signal you're stepping out

### Subject Line Formats

**Standard:** `Intro: [Name A] <> [Name B]`
**Descriptive:** `Intro: [Name A] <> [Name B] re [topic]`
**With companies:** `Intro: [Name] ([Company]) <> [Name] ([Company])`

Keep it brief, clear, and searchable. Never just "Intro" or "Quick intro."

### Template

```
Hi [Name A] and [Name B],

It's a pleasure to introduce you.

[Name A] ( [LinkedIn URL] ) [is/leads/founded] [role/company]. [Key accomplishment
or context — be specific, not generic]. [Your relationship to them]. [Optional:
specific superpower or notable detail].

[Name B] ( [LinkedIn URL] ) [is/leads/founded] [role/company]. [Key accomplishment
or context]. [Your relationship to them]. [Optional: specific superpower or notable
detail].

[Context paragraph: Why this connection makes sense. Reference a recent conversation
if possible. What the opportunity or need is. Include honest caveats if uncertain
about fit.]

Over to you,

[sign-off]
```

### Person Description Formula

For each person, include (in roughly this order):

1. **Name + LinkedIn link** — Always include when available
2. **Current role/company** — Lead with what they do now
3. **Key accomplishments** — Specific and concrete, not generic praise. Quantify when possible ("grew to 70M users", "raised $10M Series A")
4. **Your relationship** — Always be explicit ("friend", "worked together for 3 years", "met at [conference]")
5. **Unique superpower** (optional but powerful) — A vivid, specific descriptor ("wizard at prompt engineering", "one of the sharpest product thinkers I know")

### Context Paragraph

The "why this makes sense" paragraph should:

- **Reference a recent conversation** when possible ("In our last chat, you mentioned...")
- **Explain the opportunity/need** clearly and specifically
- **Include honest caveats** when uncertain ("It's hard to know whether this is a good fit, but...", "I haven't checked whether [Name] is still looking for...")
- **State the value proposition** for both sides — why would each person want this connection?

### Tone Guidelines

**DO:**
- Be warm but professional
- Use specific, concrete details (name the project, the metric, the tech stack)
- Be honest — acknowledge uncertainty or conflicts of interest
- Keep paragraphs short (1-3 sentences)
- Use active voice ("Viktor has assembled..." not "A team was assembled...")

**DON'T:**
- Use generic praise ("great person", "talented professional")
- Oversell or exaggerate capabilities
- Hide conflicts of interest or business relationships
- Make promises about outcomes
- Write walls of text

### Transparency & Honesty

Always disclose when relevant:
- You have a business relationship with one party
- You haven't worked with someone personally (yet)
- You're uncertain about fit or availability
- There's a potential conflict of interest

## After the Introduction

### BCC Switcheroo

When *replying* to an intro email, move the introducer to BCC on your first reply. Say it explicitly:

> "Thanks for the kind introduction, [Connector Name] — moving you to BCC."

This is widely considered a hallmark of professional email etiquette.

### Who Replies First

The person who *asked* for the introduction (or the more junior person) should reply first.

### Close the Loop

**Always circle back to the connector** after the meeting happens:
- Tell them how it went
- Share a few key points or next steps
- Thank them genuinely

Chris Fralic (First Round Capital): *"Nine out of 10 people don't follow through. Not only does the relationship stop there, but I'll never use the word 'reliable' to describe them."*

## The Forwardable Email Pattern

An alternative approach: instead of the connector writing the full intro, the person seeking the introduction writes a short email *addressed to the target recipient* that the connector can simply forward with a line of endorsement.

### How It Works

1. The person seeking the intro writes a short email addressed to the target person
2. They send it to the connector, asking them to forward it along
3. The connector adds 1-2 sentences of endorsement at the top, then forwards

### Forwardable Email Template

```
Subject: Intro TO [Target Name] FROM [Your Name], [Company]

Hi [Target Name],

[Your Name] here — I [role] at [Company] ( [URL] ). [2-3 sentences on what you do
and why it's relevant]. [1-2 sentences on traction/credibility].

I'd love to connect because [specific reason related to them]. Would you be open to
a quick 15-minute call?

[sign-off]
```

**Key rules:** Keep it under 250 words. Address the email to the *final recipient*. Include traction/credibility facts. Make it possible for the connector to forward in 15 seconds or less.

## Common Scenarios

### Contractor/Hire Introduction
- Lead with technical capabilities and specific tech stack
- Reference their team or consulting company
- State the hiring need clearly

### Founder-to-Founder
- Lead with company/role
- Mention specific parallel experiences or challenges
- Keep it peer-to-peer

### Service Provider Introduction
- Lead with expertise and longevity of relationship
- Explain the specific need
- Note personal experience when relevant

### Proactive/Future Introduction
- Explain you're connecting them now to avoid urgency later
- Acknowledge the speculative nature
- Focus on potential alignment

## Agent Instructions

- **IMPORTANT**: Draft the email first, never send without user approval
- Look up LinkedIn profiles if URLs are provided to get accurate titles/context
- Search prior communications (email, Slack, CRM) for relevant context about both parties
- Make both parties look good in the introduction — balanced treatment
- Be clear about why the connection makes sense
- Ask the user for missing context rather than guessing
- **Never fabricate** details about people, roles, or accomplishments
- Use HTML formatting when creating Gmail drafts (`isHtml: true`)
